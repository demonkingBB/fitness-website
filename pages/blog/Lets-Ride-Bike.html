HEY over 40, let's ride a bike!

By Julio Mendez • May 31, 2026 • 5 min read

Why I got a Bicycle at 42! I bought my bicycle two years ago when I was 42 years old. At the time, I was dealing with frequent knee pain, discomfort in my ankles, and occasional lower back pain. Like many people, I assumed it was simply part of getting older. Looking back, age was only part of the equation. Before cycling became a regular part of my routine, most of my cardio came from walking, slow jogging, the elliptical, and the StairMaster. While those exercises certainly have benefits, I cannot blame my joint pain entirely on them. The bigger issue was how I approached my weight training. I trained with good form and focused on full range of motion, but I was often using more weight than my body was ready to recover from. My muscles could handle the workload, but my ligaments, tendons, and joints were not getting the same opportunity to recover. This was especially true for my legs. As a competitive bodybuilder, I knew my legs needed more development to match my upper body. My goal is to step on stage again in the summer of 2027 with a more balanced physique. Unfortunately, my desire to bring up my weak points sometimes caused me to push harder than my recovery capacity allowed. The bicycle changed that.

1. What did Cycling do for me?

Cycling gave me a way to improve my cardiovascular fitness while placing significantly less impact on my knees and ankles compared to jogging. Instead of finishing cardio sessions feeling beaten up, I was able to get my heart rate up, burn calories, and promote blood flow without adding more stress to already fatigued joints. What surprised me most was how much better I felt overall. My knees became less aggravated, my conditioning improved, and I was able to recover more effectively between leg workouts. Cycling did not replace strength training, but it became a valuable tool that supported it. Within a few months, I found myself enjoying cardio more than I had in years. Instead of feeling like a chore, cycling became something I actually looked forward to doing. The reduction in joint discomfort was an unexpected bonus.

Quick Tip: If you're currently in a hypertrophy phase, don't automatically assume that all cardio will reduce muscle growth. Moderate cycling can actually contribute to quad development because of the repetitive leg drive involved. The key is balancing volume and recovery. If your goal is leg growth, be careful not to turn your cycling sessions into additional leg workouts that leave your quads constantly fatigued. One benefit I noticed was improved recovery in my calves, hamstrings, and glutes through increased blood flow and movement without excessive impact. Remember: More work is not always better. Recovery is where growth happens.

2. Where did I start? What was my first step?

One of the biggest lessons I learned after 40 is that progress is not just about training harder. It is about training intelligently. Recovery matters. Joint health matters. Choosing the right form of cardio matters.For me, the bicycle was not just another piece of fitness equipment. It became a way to stay active, protect my joints, and continue pursuing my bodybuilding goals without constantly fighting pain. Adding cycling gave me a sustainable way to improve cardiovascular health, support recovery, and remain active without constantly beating up my joints. If you're over 40 and struggling with knee, ankle, or lower back discomfort, consider whether your body needs a different approach rather than simply more effort. Sometimes progress comes from training smarter, not harder.
