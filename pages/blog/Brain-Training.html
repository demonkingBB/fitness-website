Brain Training Facts

By Julio Mendez • June 20, 2026 • 5 min read

1. Forget about crosswords

For decades, the standard advice for anyone worried about "senior moments" was simple: do more Sudoku, read books, and maybe try a crossword puzzle. While those are great hobbies, the latest scientific research has completely flipped the hierarchy of brain health on its head. It turns out that skeletal muscle mass and muscular strength are actually the primary predictors of how well your memory and "executive function" will hold up as you get older. If you want to protect your brain, it might be time to trade the pencil for a pair of dumbbells. Here is why your muscles are your brain's best friend.

Quick Tip:The good news is that it is never too late to start building your "cognitive reserve." Large-scale studies show that even the very elderly benefit from weight training.

• Resistance Training: Aim for 2 to 3 days per week of strength training.

• Check Your Strength: Functional markers like handgrip strength and your "sit-to-stand" speed are excellent indicators of your current brain resilience.

• Nutrition: Ensure you have enough Vitamin D and protein to support muscle maintenance. By preserving your muscle today, you aren't just staying fit; you are building the literal, structural foundation of your brain for tomorrow.

2. Muscle Brain Axis

Scientists now view your muscles as much more than just a way to move around; they are actually an endocrine organ and a metabolic reservoir. When your muscles contract—especially during resistance training—they release specialized signaling proteins called myokines directly into your bloodstream. These myokines cross the blood-brain barrier and act like a customized "repair kit" for your gray matter. BDNF: Miracle-Gro for Your Neurons. One of the most important chemicals released by your muscles is BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor). In the medical community, it is often called "Miracle-Gro for the brain". BDNF literally drives neurogenesis, which is the birth of new neurons in the hippocampus—the part of your brain responsible for memory.Another heavy hitter is Irisin. Released during intensive muscular contraction, Irisin enters the brain to clear out pro-inflammatory markers and protect your synapses from the beta-amyloid plaques that are the hallmark of Alzheimer's disease.

3. Important don't forget.

Beyond just releasing "growth" chemicals, healthy muscle mass acts as a metabolic anchor. Your muscles are your body's primary "sink" for clearing out glucose. When muscle mass drops (a condition known as sarcopenia), insulin resistance spikes. This is bad news for the brain: high insulin can damage the blood-brain barrier and impair the brain's ability to regulate its own energy, leaving your neurons defenseless against damage.

Your Brain's Supporting Cast: Vitamin D and Sleep To keep this muscle-brain connection working, you need a few key "gatekeepers." Vitamin D is essential because it actively preserves your muscle mass and strength while simultaneously minimizing cognitive dysfunction. If you are deficient in Vitamin D, your "muscle-brain axis" can't function properly. Additionally, you have to prioritize sleep. Think of sleep as the "dishwasher" for your brain. While your muscles provide the structural "armor" and chemicals for brain health, deep sleep powers the glymphatic system, which clears out the metabolic exhaust and toxic proteins (like tau) that build up during the day.
